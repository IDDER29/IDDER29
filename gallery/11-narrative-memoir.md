# I Started With `malloc`

The first time I wrote a memory allocator, I got it wrong. Not slightly wrong — catastrophically, segfault-on-the-second-call wrong. I spent a day debugging before I found it: a single pointer offset miscalculated by four bytes.

That's C. That's also how I learned to read code the way a writer edits prose: slowly, assuming nothing, trusting nothing.

---

I'm Idder. I grew up in Agadir, Morocco, and I learned to program at 1337 School — the 42 Network campus here — where the pedagogy is simple: here is the problem, no lectures, no teachers, go. You either figure it out or you ask someone who just figured it out yesterday.

I wrote a shell from scratch. A printf. A merge sort, then a radix sort, then a comparison between the two. I wrote a library. I broke things and fixed them and broke them differently and fixed them again. And at some point I looked up and realised I wasn't learning anymore — I was engineering.

---

The jump from C to React felt like stepping out of a cave. Everything was already built. The browser gave you memory management, the DOM gave you the display layer, npm gave you everything else. I thought: this is too easy.

Then I tried to build something good.

Good UI is not easy. Good conversion rates are not easy. Making a user trust you — making them feel, before they've read a word, that the people behind this product are competent and worth their time — that requires everything. It requires the typographic judgment of an editor, the information architecture of a systems designer, and the copy instincts of someone who has actually sat across from a confused user.

I learned that building [Arpio Architects](https://github.com/IDDER29/arpio-architects-case-study). It changed how I think about frontend work.

---

[Social Compass](https://github.com/IDDER29/social-compass-mobile) was different. Mobile is humbling. The device is in someone's hand. Their patience is shorter than you think. The network is worse than you planned for. Every millisecond of latency is felt. I shipped it anyway, and it runs, and people use it.

That's the thing I keep coming back to: shipping. The feature that exists is worth more than ten perfect ideas in a document.
