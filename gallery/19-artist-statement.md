# Statement of Practice

*Idder Ait Elmouden, Engineer*

</div>

---

My work begins with the belief that software is not neutral. Every API is an argument about what should be easy. Every interface is a claim about what users deserve. Every architecture decision is a value judgment wearing technical clothing.

I trained at 1337 School (42 Network, Agadir) — an environment where the pedagogy is osmotic rather than didactic. You learn by attempting, failing publicly, being corrected by peers who are also failing and learning. It is an unusual education. It produces engineers who can sit with uncertainty longer than most, because uncertainty was the classroom.

I work across the stack. Not because specialisation is wrong, but because the interesting problems live at the seam between layers — the moment where the frontend assumption collides with the backend reality, or where the design intent meets the technical constraint. I have learned that the best solutions to these problems require fluency in both languages.

The question I ask before I write a line of code: *what does success look like from the user's side of this?* Not "does the function return the correct value" but "does this experience feel inevitable to the person going through it?" The technically correct solution and the humanly correct solution are not always the same. Knowing which one is needed is the work.

The projects I'm most proud of — [Arpio Architects](https://github.com/IDDER29/arpio-architects-case-study), [Social Compass](https://github.com/IDDER29/social-compass-mobile) — are not proud of their code. They are proud of their function. The code is in service of something; the something is what matters. I try to remember this when I am tempted to be clever instead of clear.
